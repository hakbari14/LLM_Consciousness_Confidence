Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly being deployed in real-world applications, making the reliable estimation of their confidence a critical requirement. Accurate confidence assessment enables users and downstream systems to distinguish between reliable and potentially erroneous responses, thereby improving trustworthiness, safety, and decision-making.

Existing confidence estimation methods primarily rely on probabilistic measures such as token-level probabilities, entropy-based metrics, and their various extensions. Although these approaches provide useful uncertainty signals, they often fail to capture higher-level cognitive characteristics that may contribute to confidence judgments.

Since confidence is commonly regarded as an important component of conscious cognition, this research investigates confidence estimation from a consciousness-inspired perspective. Specifically, we propose a set of novel confidence metrics for Large Language Models based on principles derived from Integrated Information Theory (IIT). The proposed framework aims to quantify the degree of information integration within the model's reasoning process and explore its relationship with the reliability and confidence of generated responses.

Through extensive experiments on benchmark datasets, we evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed IIT-based confidence measures and compare them with conventional confidence estimation approaches. The results provide new insights into the connection between information integration, model confidence, and the broader study of machine consciousness.
